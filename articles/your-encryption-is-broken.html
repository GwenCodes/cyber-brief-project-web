Your Encryption Is Already Broken.

You Just Don't Know It Yet.

Your encryption, as robust as it might seem today, is on a ticking clock. The data being collected now could be the key to unlocking future vulnerabilities, compromising your privacy, security, and trust in the digital world. Proactive preparation, understanding the risks, and embracing the transition to post-quantum cryptography are not optional; they are essential steps to safeguarding your digital future.

Somewhere right now, on a server you will never see, in a country you will never visit, someone is copying your data.

Not reading it. Copying it.

They can't break the encryption. Not yet. So they're doing something smarter. They're stealing the locked box and putting it on a shelf. Waiting.

This is real. It has a name: Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL). And the deadline on that shelf just got a lot shorter.

I want to show you why.

The Long Game of Espionage

A nation-state actor, and we're talking about governments with unlimited budgets and no rush, intercepts your encrypted traffic today. Emails. Financial records. Medical files. Trade secrets. They can't unlock any of it right now.

But they don't need to unlock it now. They will unlock it eventually.

This isn't hypothetical. It's already happening. Salt Typhoon, a Chinese state-linked hacking group, spent years quietly embedded inside major U.S. telecom networks, including AT&T, Verizon, and T-Mobile, collecting call records, metadata, and communications traffic largely undetected [1]. Security researchers have pointed to exactly this kind of sustained, patient access as a textbook harvest now, decrypt later setup: the value isn't in what the attacker can read today, it's in the archive they're quietly building for tomorrow.

They wait. Five years. Ten. Long enough for the math to catch up.

And the math is catching up.

The Quantum Problem

Every lock on the internet, the kind that protects your bank login, your company's servers, your government's classified files, is built on one simple idea: some math problems are too hard for computers to solve quickly.

Factoring enormous prime numbers. That's the whole foundation of RSA, named for the three mathematicians, Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman, who invented it, the system running underneath most of the secure web.

A classical computer would need longer than the age of the universe to crack it.

A quantum computer does not.

There's an algorithm. It's called Shor's algorithm, a set of steps that lets a quantum computer find the hidden factors buried inside a massive number almost immediately, instead of grinding through them one at a time, the way a classical computer has to. And a quantum computer running it doesn't need centuries. It can solve the problems in hours.

Hours to unlock what we've spent decades building walls around.

There's a second algorithm, Grover's algorithm, that goes after symmetric encryption too, the stuff protecting files at rest. Instead of solving the math problem outright, it speeds up the guessing, letting a quantum computer search through all the possible keys roughly twice as fast as brute force normally allows.

That sounds modest, but in cryptography, halving the effective key length is a real blow. Take AES-128, a standard many companies still rely on to lock down files and databases. Picture it as a single key made up of 128 tiny switches, each one flipped on or off. Guessing the exact combination by brute force alone would take longer than anyone could stomach waiting. A quantum computer running Grover's algorithm cuts that search space in half, effectively turning that key into something closer to AES-64, a lock a serious, well-funded attacker could plausibly force open. It doesn't break symmetric encryption outright, but it cuts its strength in half. A key that felt uncrackable suddenly isn't.

The Government Response

On June 22nd, 2026, the White House signed Executive Order 14412: Securing the Nation Against Advanced Cryptographic Attacks [2].

Read that title again. Attacks. Present tense. Not "attacks that might happen." Attacks that are happening.

The order sets two hard deadlines.

By December 31st, 2030, every federal agency has to move to quantum-resistant encryption for anything establishing a secure connection.

By December 31st, 2031: quantum-resistant digital signatures. The systems that verify a document, a software update, or a message are actually what it claims to be.

Why This Is Personal

Now, you might be thinking: that's a federal problem. I'm not the Pentagon. Here's why that thinking is wrong.

The standards the government sets don't stay in Washington. They move into banks. Into hospitals. Into cloud storage providers. Into the apps on your phone. When the federal government mandates a security standard, the entire industry eventually follows, because nobody wants to be the vendor still running on broken math.

And the data being harvested right now isn't limited to classified cables and defense contracts. It's your medical records. Your financial history. Your login credentials. Every one of those is sitting in someone's encrypted archive today, waiting for a key that doesn't exist yet.

When that key arrives, and intelligence agencies increasingly believe it will arrive within this decade, everything harvested becomes readable. All at once.

Not just your secrets. The whole system's trust breaks down. If a digital signature can be forged, how do you know a software update is real? How do you know the email from your bank is actually from your bank?

That's not a data breach. That's a trust collapse.

The Response

The good news: there is a fix in motion.

That fix has a name: post-quantum cryptography. New algorithms, standardized by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), the federal agency that sets the technical rules the rest of the industry follows, built specifically to resist both classical and quantum attacks [3]. Think of it as replacing every lock in a building, all at once, before the new master key exists to open the old ones. The problem isn't whether this fix works. It's whether it arrives in time.

Preparing for the Quantum Future

For businesses, this means investing in cybersecurity expertise and planning for the inevitable migration to quantum-resistant solutions. Ask your providers directly when they're moving to post-quantum standards, and hold them to a date.

For individuals, it means being mindful of the data you share online and choosing services that prioritize strong, forward-thinking security.

The data being taken today is the data that gets read tomorrow. The only question left is whether your defenses are ready before that day arrives.
