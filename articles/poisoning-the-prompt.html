Strategic Briefing

Poisoning the Prompt:

Why Your Deception Strategy Must Target Models,
Not Humans

For decades, active deception in cybersecurity meant one thing: trick the human. Build a believable honeypot, wait for a curious attacker to poke around, and catch them in the act. The whole game was social engineering in reverse, and it worked reasonably well when there was an actual person on the other end making judgment calls. That assumption is now obsolete.

They're Not Hacking You Anymore. They're Feeding Your Data to a Model.

According to CrowdStrike's Global Threat Report 2026, AI-enabled attacks increased 89% in 2025, with threat actors using LLMs to plan and accelerate reconnaissance operations, create phishing campaigns in multiple languages, and bypass security tool safeguards at scale.

In September 2025, Anthropic detected and disrupted what they assessed as the first largely AI-orchestrated cyber espionage campaign, where a Chinese state-sponsored group used Claude Code to execute approximately 80 to 90 percent of tactical attack operations autonomously. The humans were barely in the loop. (Source: Infosecurity Magazine, Red Canary)

So if their reconnaissance is automated, your deception needs to stop targeting humans too.

Your Honeypot Does Not Fool Models

Here's the uncomfortable truth about most deception infrastructure: it was designed for a threat actor who reads output, pauses, and thinks. Default-configured honeypots, predictable decoy structures, static fake credentials, these things get flagged and skipped by automated scanners in seconds.

The goal now is different. You're not trying to confuse a person; you're trying to corrupt a pipeline. Adversaries are prompting models to generate detailed organizational hierarchies, map third-party relationships, and automate intelligence gathering that would previously have required significant manual effort. That data gets fed into the attacker's LLM, which synthesizes it into an attack plan. If you can poison what goes in, you degrade what comes out, and that's where active deception gets genuinely interesting. (Source: Google Cloud)

Turning Their Automation Against Them

Attackers today use AI to scan your systems and find weaknesses, automatically, at scale, faster than any human team could. But that automation has a vulnerability of its own: it trusts what it finds. So what if what it finds is fake?

Defenders can set up decoy environments that look completely real, fake passwords, fake system endpoints, fake misconfigurations, but lead nowhere. The attacker's AI scans, finds what looks like a goldmine, and reports back. Their team plans an attack around it. And none of it exists.

Every fake credential you plant doesn't just waste their time. It quietly corrupts everything their AI thinks it knows about you.

It gets better. AI models break down when they're fed bad information. Give them conflicting or fabricated data and their conclusions become unreliable, not sometimes, but consistently. That's just how they work. So every fake credential and phantom vulnerability you plant doesn't just waste their time. It quietly poisons everything their model thinks it knows about you.

By the time they realize something is wrong, your security team has already seen them coming. (Sources: CSO Online, Hadrian)

The Advantage of a Signal Only an Attacker Can Trigger

Ask any SOC analyst what their biggest problem is, and they'll tell you it's volume. Too many alerts, too little context, too much time spent chasing false positives. Generic monitoring tools generate noise almost by design because they're watching for anomalies in behavior that legitimate users also exhibit.

Deception assets invert this completely. Real users don't browse hidden network shares. They don't click on fake database tokens buried three directories deep. They don't authenticate against decoy credentials that were never distributed internally. So when something does interact with those assets, you don't need to run it through a correlation engine or wait for an analyst to triage the ticket. It's malicious, full stop, and your team can act on it immediately.

Why Deception Is Cheaper Than Recovery

Let's be direct about the business case, because this conversation eventually lands on a CFO's desk. Automated attacks don't move slowly. Once an agent finds a foothold and begins lateral movement, the window for containment shrinks fast. The dwell time between initial access and significant damage is measured in hours, not days, when the attacker is an autonomous agent with no sleep schedule.

Deception assets contain that risk by drawing automated tools into controlled environments where they can be observed and studied, rather than allowed to move freely toward production systems. caught at the decoy layer are fundamentally cheaper to remediate than incidents caught. And the intelligence you gather from watching a live agent interact with your honeypot, what it targets first, how it authenticates, what data it attempts to exfiltrate, is genuinely valuable for hardening your real environment.

Make It Part of the Architecture

Here's where a lot of organizations stumble: they treat deception technology as a one-time deployment rather than a living component of their security posture. A honeypot that doesn't evolve gets learned. An attacker who hits the same decoy pattern twice will eventually recognize it, especially if their tooling is adaptive.

Deception needs to be written into your risk governance from the start, not bolted on after a breach forces the conversation. Every new enterprise LLM instance your organization deploys is a natural opportunity to embed honeytokens as silent tripwires for unauthorized model access. Every application deployment workflow should have deception checkpoints built in. Wiz's practical security guidance for 2026 specifically highlights CI/CD honeytokens as high-signal detection mechanisms, citing a real-world incident where a canary AWS key triggered an alert during a compromised GitHub Action. That's exactly the kind of early warning that prevents a bad day from becoming a very bad quarter. (Source: Wiz)
