Cyber Insurance: What Executives Think It Covers vs. What It Actually Does

The bottom line up front: Most organizations discover the gaps in their cyber insurance policy at exactly the wrong moment — after a breach. Understanding what your policy actually covers is a governance decision, not an IT decision.

Cyber insurance has become a boardroom staple. In a world of rising breach costs and regulatory pressure, executives understandably treat it as a safety net. The problem is that many policies are purchased with assumptions that don't survive contact with reality.

The most common misconception is that cyber insurance functions like general liability — that if something goes wrong, the policy pays. In practice, most policies are riddled with conditions. Coverage can be denied if the insurer determines that basic security controls weren't in place at the time of the incident. "Basic controls" is often defined in the fine print, not the sales conversation, and may include things like multi-factor authentication across all systems, encrypted backups, and documented incident response plans. If your organization wasn't meeting those standards when the breach occurred, your claim may be rejected.

A second blind spot is the distinction between first-party and third-party coverage. First-party covers your own losses — recovery costs, business interruption, forensic investigation. Third-party covers claims made against you by customers or partners whose data was compromised. Many executives assume they have both. Not all policies include both.

Ransomware coverage deserves particular scrutiny. Some policies exclude ransom payments entirely. Others cap them well below what attackers typically demand. And increasingly, insurers are requiring organizations to demonstrate they have offline, tested backups before ransomware coverage will apply — because an organization that can restore from backup has less reason to pay, which reduces the insurer's exposure.

The executive action here isn't to become a policy expert. It's to ask your security and legal teams three specific questions: What does our policy actually exclude? What security controls are we required to maintain to keep coverage valid? And when did we last test whether our incident response process meets the insurer's requirements?

CASE STUDY: WHEN THE POLICY DIDN'T PAY

A mid-sized logistics company suffered a ransomware attack that encrypted its core operations platform and took the business offline for eleven days. The financial impact — lost revenue, recovery costs, and emergency IT support — exceeded $4 million. The company had cyber insurance with a stated limit well above that figure.

The claim was denied.

The insurer's investigation found that the company had not implemented multi-factor authentication on its remote access systems, a control explicitly listed as a coverage requirement in the policy. The organization's IT team had been aware of the gap and had flagged it internally. It was on a remediation roadmap. It had not been prioritized.

The denial triggered a secondary crisis. With no insurance payout, the company was forced to draw on credit facilities to cover recovery costs, which strained relationships with lenders and delayed a planned acquisition. The CISO left the organization within six months. The story eventually became public through litigation when the company challenged the denial — and lost.

What makes this case instructive isn't the ransomware attack itself. Ransomware happens. What's instructive is the chain of decisions that made it catastrophic: a policy purchased without a full controls audit, a security gap that was known but deprioritized, and an executive team that assumed coverage meant protection.

The question this case leaves for any executive is a simple one: do you know what your policy requires you to have in place right now?

COMPLIANCE DEADLINES EVERY EXECUTIVE NEEDS TO KNOW IN 2026

next three months are unusually dense with regulatory activity. This is not a compliance team problem — several of these deadlines carry board-level accountability.

February — EU AI Act Guidance Published. High-risk AI system classifications are being clarified. If your organization uses AI in hiring, credit decisions, customer-facing services, or operational management, you need to know whether those systems fall under high-risk designation and what obligations follow.

March 23-26 — RSA Conference, San Francisco. Not a compliance deadline, but the moment the industry sets its agenda. Expect significant announcements around regulatory interpretation, vendor capabilities, and emerging threat intelligence. Worth tracking even for executives who don't attend.

April 18 — NIS2 Enforcement Milestone. Organizations in scope must have active cybersecurity measures in place and be ready for regulatory inspection. This applies to a significantly broader range of sectors than its predecessor, including mid-sized organizations in energy, transport, healthcare, digital infrastructure, and more. If you have European operations and haven't completed a NIS2 gap assessment, this date is urgent.

Q1-Q2 — DORA Documentation Deadlines. Financial entities operating in the EU must have their ICT Risk Management Framework documentation finalized. This is not a future-planning exercise — inspectors will ask to see it.

~May — HIPAA Security Rule Overhaul Final Rule Expected. The first major update to HIPAA's security provisions in over a decade. Healthcare organizations and their business associates should be preparing for new requirements around access controls, encryption, and incident response.
