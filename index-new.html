Your Encryption Is Already Broken. You Just Don't Know It Yet.

Nation-state hackers aren't trying to crack today's encryption. They're collecting it now, waiting for quantum computers to unlock it later.

Somewhere right now, on a server you will never see, in a country you will never visit, someone is copying your data.

Not reading it. Copying it.

They can't break the encryption. Not yet. So they're doing something smarter. They're stealing the locked box and putting it on a shelf. Waiting.

"Harvest now, decrypt later."

This is real. It has a name: harvest now, decrypt later. And the deadline on that shelf just got a lot shorter.

I want to show you why.

The Long Game of Espionage

A nation-state actor—and we're talking about governments with unlimited budgets and no rush—intercepts your encrypted traffic today. Emails. Financial records. Medical files. Trade secrets.

They can't unlock any of it right now.

But they don't need to unlock it now. They will unlock it eventually.

This isn't hypothetical. It's already happening. Salt Typhoon, a Chinese state-linked hacking group, spent years quietly embedded inside major U.S. telecom networks, including AT&T, Verizon, and T-Mobile, collecting call records, metadata, and communications traffic largely undetected [1].

Security researchers have pointed to exactly this kind of sustained, patient access as a textbook harvest now, decrypt later setup: the value isn't in what the attacker can read today—it's in the archive they're quietly building for tomorrow. [2]

They wait. Five years. Ten. Long enough for the math to catch up.

And the math is catching up.

The Quantum Problem

Every lock on the internet—the kind that protects your bank login, your company's servers, and government systems—is built on one simple idea: some math problems are too hard for computers to solve quickly.

Factoring enormous prime numbers. That's the foundation of RSA, named after Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman.

A classical computer would need longer than the age of the universe to crack it.

A quantum computer does not.

Shor's algorithm allows a quantum computer to find the hidden factors inside massive numbers exponentially faster than classical computers. Instead of centuries, the problem could be solved in hours.

Hours to unlock what we've spent decades building walls around.

A second breakthrough, Grover's algorithm, targets symmetric encryption by dramatically reducing brute-force search time.

AES-128, one of today's most common encryption standards, effectively behaves more like AES-64 against a sufficiently powerful quantum computer.

It doesn't completely break symmetric encryption, but it cuts its effective strength in half.

The Government Response

Here's where this stops being theoretical.

On June 22, 2026, the White House signed Executive Order 14412: Securing the Nation Against Advanced Cryptographic Attacks. [3]

Read that title again. Attacks. Present tense.

The order establishes two deadlines:

  • December 31, 2030 — Federal agencies must migrate secure connections to quantum-resistant encryption.
  • December 31, 2031 — Federal agencies must adopt quantum-resistant digital signatures.

Why This Is Personal

You might think this only affects governments. It doesn't.

Federal cybersecurity standards eventually become banking standards, healthcare standards, cloud-provider standards, and consumer technology standards.

The information being harvested today isn't limited to classified government documents.

  • Medical records
  • Financial history
  • Login credentials
  • Business communications
  • Trade secrets

Every one of those may already exist inside someone's encrypted archive.

When practical quantum decryption arrives, everything collected becomes readable.

That's not simply a data breach. It's a collapse of digital trust.

If digital signatures can be forged, how do you know a software update is genuine? Or that an email from your bank is actually from your bank?

The Response

Fortunately, the solution already exists.

It's called post-quantum cryptography (PQC).

New algorithms standardized by NIST are specifically designed to resist both classical and quantum attacks. [4]

Think of it as replacing every lock in a building before someone manufactures the master key.

Cloud providers are deploying it. Browsers are adopting it. Enterprises are beginning the migration.

But large-scale cryptographic transitions take years—not months.

Preparing for the Quantum Future

Businesses should begin planning their migration to post-quantum cryptography today. Ask vendors when they will support quantum-resistant standards and request timelines.

Individuals should remain mindful of the data they share online and choose services committed to modern cryptographic protections.

This is no longer science fiction.

It has an Executive Order number. It has deadlines.

2030.
2031.

The data being stolen today is the data that gets read tomorrow.

The only question left is whether your defenses are ready before that day arrives.
